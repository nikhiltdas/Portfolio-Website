Where I truly live

Malayalam literature is not a subject I study — it is the soil I was born from. The language of my identity runs through everything I do: my teaching, my making, even the way I read.

I believe that a poem written in Kerala's tradition will have a odour of our elite spices carries as much philosophical weight as any Western canon. Our aksharas — the syllables of Malayalam — are among the oldest sounds of human civilization, and every time I pick up a book in that script, I feel that continuity as a living thing.

Literature is my first language; everything else — circuits, code, classrooms — is a kind of translation of what I first understood through words.

Who I am

Born in Kerala,
Wired to the World

I'm a STEM educator, maker, and reader rooted in Kottayam, Kerala. My days are spent soldering circuits with eight-year-olds in the morning and reading Basheer in the evening — and I see no contradiction in that.

My education in computer applications gave me formal tools; growing up in Kerala's literary and cultural richness gave me the vision for what those tools should serve. The most interesting work happens at the collision of disciplines.

Running Circuitbay, building IoT systems for public sanitation, designing pedagogy for the very young — all of it is, at some level, storytelling: trying to communicate something true about the world to someone else.

Education

Teaching the art of
failing better

My pedagogy for early childhood STEM is built on a conviction: the most important thing a child can learn is how to be wrong and try again.

"A classroom that celebrates failure is one that trusts children to be fully human."

I design experiences for ages 3–8 that present real-world constraints, encourage cross-domain problem solving, and resist the urge to give answers before questions are genuinely felt.

I call this the street-smart polymath approach — children who can read a circuit, recite a verse, and navigate a human disagreement, all in the same afternoon.

Art & Heritage

Painted in the colors of
Kerala

Kerala's visual language is among the most sophisticated in the world. The mural traditions of Mattancherry and Padmanabhapuram, the fluid geometry of Kathakali's costume, the ochre and green of temple architecture — these are not decorations but a complete worldview rendered in pigment and form.

I grew up in the shadow of a nalukettu, where architecture itself encoded values: the central courtyard open to the sky, the hierarchy of spaces, the way light entered at particular hours. That spatial philosophy shapes how I design classrooms, dashboards, and poems.

Art, for me, is not aesthetic pleasure alone. It is memory made visible — the same impulse that makes me keep a reading journal, solder a sensor, or write a podcast script.
